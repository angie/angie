# AI Policy

## How I use AI

I use AI tools daily for coding, research, and automation. I have [an agentic setup](https://github.com/angie/agentic-setup) that I build in the open.

What I ship is my responsibility. AI can generate the code much quicker (and, let's be real, mostly at a higher quality) than I can, but I own the outcome. If it breaks, that's on me, not the model. I must review and understand anything that goes out as client work.

## What I won't do

- Ever create, use, or share AI-generated art.
- Pretend the tools are neutral.

## What I believe

- [Software developers are the bellwether for all knowledge work](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/2/lennys-podcast/#software-engineers-as-bellwethers-for-other-information-workers). 
- Coding is more or less a [solved problem](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/head-of-claude-code-what-happens). Software engineering isn't.
- What remains, and what's becoming more valuable, is taste and judgement: what to build, what not to build, how systems survive contact with reality, politics, and time. The role compresses upwards into product engineering. Less tippy tappy typing, more thinking.
- The cost of AI progress is being paid by the people least able to bear it. Junior developers can't get a foothold because the implementation work they'd learn on is disappearing. Artists and writers had their work scraped without consent to build tools that now compete with them. They funded their own replacement and weren't paid for the training data. The benefits accrue to companies and billionaires while the costs fall on those starting out, or those already struggling and underpaid to begin with.
- The people who decided to train models on copyrighted work without consent should be held personally and legally accountable.
- Using these tools and criticising how they were built is not a contradiction. I use AI for work, but I also believe the training practices were ethically wrong and the people who authorised them should face legal consequences.

## What I would like from collaborators and colleagues

- Be honest about your use of AI.
- Don't pass off generated work as hand-crafted.
- Don't use AI to bulk-generate low-quality contributions.
- Don't ask humans to review work you haven't put any effort into.
- Respect the creative work of others — if you didn't make it and didn't get permission, don't use it.

## Further reading

These pieces shaped my thinking:

- [**The five stages of losing our craft**](https://andrewmurphy.io/blog/the-five-stages-of-losing-our-craft) by Andrew Murphy.
- [**The Next Two Years of Software Engineering**](https://addyosmani.com/blog/next-two-years/) by Addy Osmani — concrete guidance on what junior and senior developers should actually be doing to adapt.
- [**Outcome Engineering Manifesto**](https://o16g.com/manifesto/) by Cory Ondrejka — the operational vision for how development work changes when implementation is cheap.
- [**The machines are fine. I'm worried about us**](https://ergosphere.blog/posts/the-machines-are-fine/). "The real threat is a slow, comfortable drift toward not understanding what you're doing." A long read, but a great one.
